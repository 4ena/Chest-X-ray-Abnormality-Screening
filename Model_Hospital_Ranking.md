# Model Hospital Ranking

**Tier ranking on severity of detected pulmonary pathologies for radiologist read-queue prioritization.**

---

## Tier 1 — Immediately Life-Threatening

- **Enlarged Cardiomediastinum** — May represent aortic dissection or rupture.
- **Pneumothorax** — Rapid deterioration if untreated.

## Tier 2 — Urgent (hours)

- **Edema** — Treatment window of hours.
- **Consolidation** — May represent hemorrhage; can progress rapidly.
- **Pneumonia** — Risk of sepsis or respiratory failure within hours to days.

## Tier 3 — Semi-Urgent

- **Pleural Effusion** — High 30-day mortality, but acute decline is slower than Tier 2.
- **Cardiomegaly** — Indicator of cardiac disease; rarely acutely fatal.

## Tier 4 — Moderate

- **Atelectasis** — Ranges from incidental to serious; often clinically insignificant.
- **Fracture** — Isolated rib fractures rarely acutely fatal (flail chest excluded).
- **Lung Opacity** — Nonspecific; requires clinical correlation.
- **Pleural Other** — Low acute risk.

## Tier 5 — Low / Baseline

- **Support Devices** — Ranges from no pathology to dislodged device.
- **No Finding** — No detected pathology.


#  Source Documentation
### Pneumothorax
 
- **[StatPearls – Acute Pneumothorax Evaluation and Treatment](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK538316/)**
  Tension pneumothorax described as a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate intervention. Details on hemodynamic compromise, hypoxia incidence (3–11%), and mediastinal shift mechanics.
 
- **[StatPearls – Pneumothorax](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK441885/)**
  Mortality data: secondary spontaneous pneumothorax ~10% mortality; tension pneumothorax high mortality if untreated. Recurrence rates (30–60% within 3 years). COPD patients have 3.5x relative mortality increase.
 
- **[Medscape – Pneumothorax Prognosis](https://www.medscape.com/answers/424547-67573/what-is-the-prognosis-of-pneumothorax)**
  SSP mortality 1–17% depending on underlying disease and size. COPD patients: 5% die before chest tube placed. AIDS patients: 25% inpatient mortality. Tension pneumothorax progression to respiratory insufficiency and death.
 
- **[PMC – Management of Pneumothorax in Emergency Medicine Departments](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3955499/)**
  Multicenter epidemiological data: 6.4% overall mortality rate. 82.2% male patients. Mortality higher in neonatal/infant secondary and iatrogenic pneumothorax.
 
- **[Journal of Thoracic Disease – Pneumothorax: Observation](https://jtd.amegroups.org/article/view/3083/3674)**
  Guidelines for observation vs. intervention. 79% success rate with observation alone in primary spontaneous pneumothorax. SSP requires hospitalization due to higher morbidity/mortality.
 
- **[NEJM – Pneumothorax and Air Leaks in ARDS](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJM199802053380601)**
  In ARDS patients, pneumothorax occurred in 6.9% of cases. 30-day mortality rate of 40% in the study cohort. Air leaks correlated with more severe lung disease.
 
- **[PMC – Pneumothorax in COVID-19 Patients](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8990159/)**
  Pneumothorax development identified as one of the greatest factors affecting mortality in COVID-19 ICU patients, alongside D-dimer elevation.
 ### Enlarged Cardiomediastinum / Cardiomegaly
 
- **[StatPearls – Cardiomegaly](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK542296/)**
  Key source for enlarged cardiomediastinum differential: aortic dissection, pericardial tamponade, mediastinal mass. 1-year mortality ~30%, 5-year ~50% for symptomatic heart failure. Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy mortality 1–4%.
 
- **[Radiology Masterclass – Cardiac Contour and Pulmonary Oedema](https://www.radiologymasterclass.co.uk/tutorials/chest/chest_pathology/chest_pathology_page8)**
  Radiographic criteria: cardiothoracic ratio >50% indicates cardiomegaly. Associated features: upper zone vessel enlargement, Kerley B lines, pleural effusions.
 
- **[PMC – Imaging in Acute Dyspnea](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10077421/)**
  Five CT signs sufficient to assess acute heart failure risk: enlarged heart, bilateral septal thickening, bilateral pleural effusion, increased vascular diameter, bilateral ground-glass opacities. Two or more signs make AHF diagnosis near-certain.
 
### Pulmonary Edema
 
- **[StatPearls – Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK544260/)**
  High short-term mortality rate emphasized. Can cause severe hypoxia, end-organ damage, multi-organ failure. Treatment protocols: IV diuretics, vasodilators, inotropes.
 
- **[StatPearls – Pulmonary Edema](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK557611/)**
  ARDS mortality declined from 60% (1967–1981) to 30–40% (1990s). Troponin elevation and hypoalbuminemia as prognostic markers. Comprehensive overview of cardiogenic vs. non-cardiogenic pathophysiology.
 
- **[Wikipedia – Pulmonary Edema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_edema)**
  Large review citing 7.6% incidence with 11.9% in-hospital mortality. 50% survival rate generally associated with the condition. Flash pulmonary edema as a clinical syndrome.
 
- **[JAHA – Hospital and 4-Year Mortality in Acute Pulmonary Edema](https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/JAHA.115.002581)**
  806 patients studied. Hospital mortality 26.5–31.5%. Independent predictors: age, systolic BP on admission, APE recurrence, need for inotropics/intubation.
 
- **[ScienceDirect – Pulmonary Edema Prognostic Score](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0147956307001045)**
  PEPS scoring system: 2% in-hospital mortality for score 0, up to 64% for score 4. Key predictors: acute MI, heart rate >115, systolic BP ≤130, WBC >11,500.
 
- **[Springer – Mortality in ACPE Treated with CPAP](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00134-008-1281-7)**
  454 ACPE patients: ACPE-related mortality 3.8%, in-hospital mortality 11.4%. Predictors: advanced age, low blood pressure, low PaO2/FiO2, hypocapnia, anemia.
 
- **[ClinCaseQuest – Acute Pulmonary Edema Emergency Algorithm](https://clincasequest.hospital/acute-pulmonary-edema/)**
  1-year mortality up to 40%. 16% of acute heart failure hospitalizations present with pulmonary edema. Treatment algorithm including morphine caution and vasodilator protocol.
 
- **[PubMed – Hospital and Long-Term Survival in APE](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3604942/)**
  Hospital mortality 46% with concurrent AMI, 6% without. 70% of hospital survivors dead at 6 years.

  ### Pneumonia
 
- **[StatPearls – Community-Acquired Pneumonia](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK430749/)**
  8th leading cause of death in the US, 1st among infectious causes. ICU mortality up to 23%. CURB-65 and PSI scoring systems for severity assessment.
 
- **[StatPearls – Pneumonia Pathology](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK526116/)**
  In-hospital mortality 6.5% (~102,821 annual US deaths). 30-day mortality 13.0%, 1-year mortality 30.6%. Complications: respiratory failure, sepsis, empyema, multi-organ dysfunction.
 
- **[StatPearls – Nosocomial Pneumonia](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK535441/)**
  Ventilator-associated pneumonia mortality 20–50%. Hospital-acquired pneumonia associated with increased risk of death. Risk factors: severity of illness, shock, coma, ARDS.
 
- **[CDC NCHS – Mortality in Hospitalized Pneumonia Patients](https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/data/nhsr/nhsr167.pdf)**
  National data on in-hospital death by ICU status. Risk of death within 30 and 365 days stratified by sex, age, length of stay, and ICU admission.
 
- **[Nature – Bacterial Etiology and Mortality in Pneumonia](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-022-12904-z)**
  Hospital-acquired pneumonia mortality 1.75x higher than community-acquired. Gram-negative pathogens accounted for 60.5% of cases. P. aeruginosa most frequent in HAP/HCAP.
 
- **[PMC – Predictors of Mortality in Severe Pneumonia](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11302088/)**
  Meta-analysis of 22 studies. Key mortality predictors: septic shock, ARDS, acute kidney injury, metabolic acidosis, low PaO2/FiO2. Multi-organ failure is the primary cause of death.
- **[Springer – Pneumothorax Impact on COVID-19 Mortality](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12890-024-03161-z)**
  Among 1.57M COVID-19 patients, 1.41% developed pneumothorax with significantly higher hospitalization costs and worse outcomes.
 ### Pleural Effusion
 
- **[StatPearls – Pleural Effusion](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK448189/)**
  1.5 million US patients affected annually. Marker of increased morbidity and mortality. Pathophysiology overview: increased capillary pressure, permeability, decreased oncotic pressure.
 
- **[PMC – Mortality of Hospitalized Patients with Pleural Effusion](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4428160/)**
  15% dead at 30 days, 32% at 1 year. Severity of illness and malignancy associated with 30-day mortality. Thoracentesis may provide short-term protective effect.
 
- **[PMC – Mortality Among Patients Undergoing Thoracentesis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4857137/)**
  308 patients studied. Malignant effusion: 37% 30-day mortality, 77% 1-year. CHF-related: 22% 30-day, 53% 1-year. Bilateral effusions significantly worse prognosis.
 
- **[Frontiers – Mortality in Hospitalized Patients with PE](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2022.828783/full)**
  Prospective multicenter study. APACHE II and SOFA scores as short-term and long-term mortality predictors. CHF most common cause of pleural effusion.
 
- **[StatPearls – Malignant Pleural Effusion](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK519522/)**
  500,000 new cases/year in US and Europe. Lung and breast cancer account for >50% of cases. Massive effusion defined as occupying entire hemithorax.
 
- **[PMC – Pleural Effusion Diagnosis, Treatment, and Management](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4753987/)**
  Comprehensive review of effusion types, diagnostic criteria, Light's criteria, and treatment. Rounded atelectasis as a complication. Large effusions cause contralateral mediastinal shift.
 
### Atelectasis
 
- **[StatPearls – Atelectasis](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK545316/)**
  Incidence up to 90% in patients under general anesthesia. Can contribute to pneumonia and acute respiratory failure. Types: obstructive, compression, adhesive (surfactant-related), cicatrization. Poor evidence linking atelectasis to postoperative fever.

### Fracture, Lung Opacity, Lung Lesion, Pleural Other
 
No specific sources were needed beyond general clinical knowledge for these lower-acuity findings. Their ranking is based on the principle that they are rarely immediately life-threatening in isolation, though fracture can escalate (flail chest, associated hemothorax/pneumothorax).
